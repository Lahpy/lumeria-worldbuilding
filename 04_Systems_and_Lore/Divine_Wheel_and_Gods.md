# The Divine Wheel and the Gods

## Overview

The gods of Lumeria dwell within a sacred island or isolated divine region currently referred to as **The Divine Wheel**.

The Divine Wheel is arranged in a radial pattern. Each wedge or section belongs to one god's **domicile**. At the center stands a massive tree currently referred to as the **Crownroot**.

This creates a clear visual map structure:

```text
[Divine Domicile] [Divine Domicile] [Divine Domicile]
        \              |              /
          \            |            /
             [ The Crownroot ]
          /            |            \
        /              |              \
[Divine Domicile] [Divine Domicile] [Divine Domicile]
```

The exact number of domiciles can change over time, but **12 gods** is the current placeholder.

## Number of gods

The number of gods is not fixed forever.

A god is not simply a permanent member of an unchanging pantheon. A mortal, mythical being, or unique individual can potentially become so powerful, complete, and conceptually distinct that they are recognized as a new god with a new domain.

This requires extreme uniqueness.

Possible requirements:

- repeated Divine Acts
- extraordinary mastery
- a new domain that no existing god fully represents
- enough recognition from mortals, gods, or the divine structure itself
- survival of a transformation into true godhood

For now, the pantheon uses **12 gods** as a working placeholder.

## Domains and worship

Gods are not locked to single nations.

Different cultures, cities, industries, and communities worship whichever god fits their lives.

Examples:

- a seaside port may worship a water or sea god to bless fishing and travel
- a forge city may worship a fire, forge, or invention god
- a farming village may worship a nature or harvest god
- an academy may worship a knowledge, light, or star god
- a frontier hunter town may worship a hunt, flame, survival, or death-related god
- an art capital may worship a beauty, art, or inspiration god

This allows religion to vary naturally by geography and culture.

## Domiciles

Each god's section of the Divine Wheel is called a **domicile**.

A domicile is more than a palace. It is the god's divine household, training ground, court, ecosystem, sanctuary, and symbolic extension of their domain.

Mortals who are invited to a domicile may live there physically and cultivate their abilities under that god's authority.

Each domicile should have a distinct visual identity.

Potential examples:

- a volcanic forge palace
- a sea-glass temple surrounded by divine waters
- a white-gold sanctuary of light
- a starry observatory city
- an endless autumn garden
- a living forest court
- a marble city of art, music, and statues
- a quiet black archive of memory and death

## Divine invitations

When a person performs a **Divine Act**, a god may send a physical messenger inviting them to the relevant domicile.

The messenger should reflect the god.

Examples:

- fire god: mini phoenix, ember bird, ash-winged hawk
- sea god: water serpent, pearl-shell courier, mist heron
- star god: silver moth, star-eyed owl, falling-star letter
- nature god: deer spirit, flower-crowned fox, vine-wrapped bird
- forge god: bronze raven, clockwork beetle, hammer-marked golem
- death or memory god: black butterfly, pale crow, silent veiled attendant
- art or beauty god: songbird, living painting, dancer-spirit
- storm god: lightning falcon, cloud wolf, thunder-drake hatchling

## Trials and ranking

Entering a domicile does not automatically make someone elite.

An invited person undergoes trials to determine their rank within that divine household.

Working rank concepts:

- **Invited** — noticed by a god but not yet proven
- **Aspirant** — passed the entry trial and begins cultivation
- **Votary** — recognized servant or student of the god
- **Exemplar** — high-ranking representative of the domain
- **Paragon** — one of the strongest living followers of that god
- **Seat Candidate** — someone whose growth may eventually approach godhood

These are working names and can be replaced later.

## The Crownroot

At the center of the Divine Wheel stands the **Crownroot**.

Publicly, the gods believe the Crownroot is the origin of their magic because it heals, empowers, nourishes, and stabilizes the domiciles.

The tree's effects are real.

It may provide:

- healing
- magical nourishment
- spiritual stabilization
- growth acceleration
- divine resonance
- protection against collapse of the Divine Wheel

However, the gods may have mistaken a conduit for the source.

Secret possibility:

> The Crownroot is connected to the Aster Forest. It may be a branch, graft, cutting, echo, or surviving extension of the same source as the Aster Forest.

This truth has been lost to the gods. At most, some gods may suspect that the tree is stranger than they admit.

## Godhood and permanence

Once a being becomes a god, they are generally untouchable unless killed.

A god cannot simply be voted out or demoted by ordinary politics.

Possible rules:

- a god may lose influence but remain divine
- a god may lose worship but retain their seat
- a god may be challenged, but this is extremely rare
- a god can be killed, but this is nearly impossible
- killing a god may destabilize their domicile
- a dead god's domain may fracture, go dormant, or seek a successor

This makes the creation of a new god, or the death of an old one, a historical event.
